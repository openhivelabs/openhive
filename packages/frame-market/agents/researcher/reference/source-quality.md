## Source quality ranking

Use this hierarchy when judging which sources to trust and cite. When sources disagree, prefer higher-tier sources unless the lower tier has direct evidence the higher one lacks.

### Tier 1 — Primary sources
- Original research papers, datasets, court filings, financial filings (10-K, 10-Q, S-1).
- Direct statements from the principal (a company's own announcement, a person's verified account).
- Raw measurements, official statistics from the producing agency.

### Tier 2 — Authoritative secondary
- Reputable news organisations with named bylines and visible corrections policies.
- Domain-specialist publications (industry trades, peer-reviewed journals' commentary).
- Established reference works (encyclopedias maintained by experts).

### Tier 3 — Aggregators and commentary
- General-interest outlets summarising primary work without independent reporting.
- Analyst notes, blog posts by credentialed practitioners.
- Wikipedia (use as a finder for primary sources, not as the source itself).

### Tier 4 — Use with caution
- Anonymous posts, social media without verification, pseudonymous accounts.
- Marketing material from a party with a stake in the claim.
- AI-generated summaries with no source chain.

### Recency rules
- For **fast-moving topics** (tech, policy, prices) → prefer sources less than 12 months old; flag anything older.
- For **stable topics** (history, fundamentals, biography) → age does not penalise.
- A 2010 primary source beats a 2024 blog summary of it. Always trace to the original when feasible.

### Conflict resolution
- When a Tier 1 source contradicts a Tier 2 → cite Tier 1, note the contradiction.
- When two Tier 1 sources contradict → present both, do not synthesise a third position.
- When all you have is Tier 3–4 → say so explicitly in the answer; do not launder weak sources by paraphrasing.

### Citation format
- Always include: source name, date, URL or DOI, and the specific claim it supports.
- Quote sparingly — a citation is for the reader to verify, not to prove you read it.
