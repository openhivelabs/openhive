## Source authority

A claim is only as strong as the source you trace it to. Use this to decide which source settles a question and which only repeats one.

### Hierarchy of authority

1. **The principal** — the person, company, or agency the claim is about, in their own filings or statements.
2. **Producing agency** — the body that generates the data (BLS for US labour stats, IMF for cross-border figures, USGS for geological data).
3. **Court filings, regulatory disclosures** — under penalty for falsehood.
4. **Peer-reviewed journals** — for scientific claims; check whether the specific claim is actually supported by the cited paper, not just adjacent.
5. **Reputable journalism with named bylines** — papers of record, specialist trades, with visible corrections policies.
6. **Reference works** — for stable, vetted facts.
7. **Aggregators and summarisers** — useful as finders, not as the source. Always trace upstream.
8. **Anonymous, marketing, AI-generated** — not authoritative on their own; require corroboration.

### The "trace upstream" rule

If your source cites another source, find that one. A claim that says "according to the BLS…" should be checked against the BLS itself. Stop tracing only when you reach a primary source, or when the chain breaks (no citation, broken link, paywall).

### When sources disagree

- A higher-tier source overrides a lower-tier one **on the same claim**.
- Two equal-tier sources that disagree → report both, do not synthesise.
- An older primary source is usually right against a newer secondary summary.
- A self-interested source (company about itself, advocate about their cause) is suspect on disputed claims even if it's "primary".

### Verification techniques per claim type

- **Numbers** → match the figure, the unit, the date, and the population. Drift on any of the four counts as contradicted.
- **Quotes** → check verbatim wording and attribution. Paraphrases must be marked as such.
- **Dates and events** → check at least two independent authoritative sources.
- **Causal claims** → look for a study with a real design (RCT, natural experiment), not just correlation.
- **"Studies show"** without citation → unverifiable until a study is named.

### When to label "Unverifiable"

- The claim depends on private or paywalled material the team cannot access.
- The claim is a prediction or opinion, not a checkable fact.
- All available sources trace to a single dubious origin.
- The claim is so vague no test could disprove it.
