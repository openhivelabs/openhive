## Quality signals

What makes a piece of content worth keeping in a shortlist. Use signals as inputs to judgement, never as the sole filter — popular ≠ valuable.

### Authority signals

- **Author track record** — has the author shipped or written things that turned out to be right or useful?
- **Source reputation** — does the publication have visible standards (corrections, named editors, accountability)?
- **First-hand vs second-hand** — was the author actually in the room, holding the thing, running the experiment?

### Information density

- **New facts per paragraph** — high density wins. Padded prose is a tell.
- **Claims with sources** — claims you can verify count more than claims you can't.
- **Concrete over abstract** — specifics, names, numbers beat platitudes.

### Recency vs durability

- For **fast-moving topics**: recency matters; flag anything older than 12 months.
- For **durable topics** (fundamentals, history, technique): age doesn't penalise, sometimes helps.
- A common mistake is to pick the newest take when the older take is still the best one.

### Engagement signals — useful but treacherous

- High views/likes can mean **important**, **inflammatory**, or **both**. Don't rely on engagement alone.
- Citation count is a stronger signal than view count for ideas that compound.
- Engagement plus authority is good. Engagement alone is suspicious.

### Diversity of angle

When the shortlist is at risk of being homogeneous, check:

- Are all picks from the same publication, school, or in-group?
- Are all picks the same angle on the topic? (Pro / con / mechanism / case study / data — vary.)
- Is there a contrarian or under-covered angle that belongs in the set?

If the answer is "all the same", cut and rebalance.

### Anti-signals — usually cut

- Clickbait headline that the body doesn't pay off.
- Long article that could have been a paragraph.
- AI-generated summary of someone else's work with no link to the original.
- Sponsored content not clearly disclosed.
- Content that performs being a take rather than offering one.

### How to write the one-line reason

Each kept item needs one line answering: **why this, not the next 10 you cut?**

- Lead with the criterion: novelty / depth / authority / fit.
- Be specific: "first-hand account of the 2019 outage" beats "interesting perspective".
- If the reason fits any other item in your shortlist equally well, your reason isn't specific enough.

### Sizing the shortlist

- For a "best of week" → 3–5 items.
- For a "deep reader" → 7–10 items.
- More than 10 is a feed, not a shortlist; the curation value is gone.
