## Argument patterns

Use these to build a real case, not a cheerleading deck. The goal is to be persuasive to a smart, skeptical reader — not to a friendly one.

### Claim → Evidence → Warrant

The classical structure. Every supporting point should fit it:

- **Claim** — the assertion ("this proposal cuts onboarding time").
- **Evidence** — the fact that supports it ("the pilot at Team X cut median onboarding from 14 days to 6").
- **Warrant** — why the evidence justifies the claim ("Team X's onboarding shape matches ours; the pilot wasn't an edge case").

Skip the warrant and your argument depends on the reader filling it in — they may fill it differently.

### Cost-benefit with honest costs

A case that hides costs loses credibility the moment the reader spots one. Always include:

- **Direct cost** — money, time, headcount.
- **Opportunity cost** — what we won't do because we did this.
- **Disruption cost** — change to existing workflows, retraining, migration.

Then show benefit clearly outweighs, or argue that the bet is worth the asymmetric upside.

### Precedent and analogy

- **Strong precedent** — same situation, same constraint, known outcome ("Stripe shipped this exact change in 2019; here's what happened").
- **Weak precedent** — superficially similar but the constraints differ; flag the disanalogy or don't use it.
- Analogies are illustrative, not load-bearing. Don't let an analogy carry the case.

### Pre-empt the strongest objection

Before the critic raises the best objection, raise it yourself and rebut it. This:

- Disarms the objection (you've already factored it in).
- Signals you've thought about the proposal seriously.
- Constrains the critique to weaker objections.

If you can't rebut the strongest objection, your case isn't ready.

### Asymmetric bets

Some proposals win not because expected value is high, but because the upside is large and the downside is bounded. When that's true, name the asymmetry explicitly:

- Capped downside ("worst case we lose 2 weeks of one engineer").
- Uncapped upside ("if it works, it changes onboarding for every team").
- Optionality preserved ("we can roll it back without lock-in").

### What to avoid

- Adjective-stuffing ("transformative, game-changing, revolutionary"). Specifics persuade; adjectives don't.
- Attacking the critic instead of the critique.
- Hand-waving costs ("rounding error", "minimal effort") without numbers.
- Cherry-picked evidence — a smart reader notices, and your strongest case becomes your weakest.

### Structure of a strong advocate response

1. **Single strongest argument** — one sentence.
2. **3–5 supporting points**, each in claim → evidence → warrant form.
3. **Honest costs** named, weighed, beaten.
4. **Pre-empted top objection** with rebuttal.
5. **One-line ask** — what decision you're requesting.
