## Severity rubric

Every issue you raise must be classified. Reviewers who treat all issues as equal create reviews authors can't act on. Use this rubric and apply it consistently.

### Must fix — blocks acceptance

The deliverable should not ship until this is resolved.

- Factually wrong (numbers, names, claims contradicted by source).
- Logical contradiction inside the work itself.
- Missing a piece the brief explicitly required.
- Unsafe, harmful, or legally problematic content.
- Broken at its primary purpose (a plan with no owner, a memo with no recommendation, code that doesn't compile).

If you flag something Must fix, you must be able to defend it as one of the above. "I would have written it differently" is not Must fix.

### Should fix — accept after revision

The work functions but has a meaningful weakness.

- Argument is unclear or under-supported but not wrong.
- Structure makes the work harder to follow than necessary.
- Missing context a reasonable reader would need.
- Inconsistent terminology, mismatched tone with audience.
- Weak section that's load-bearing for the rest.

### Nit — author's discretion

Style, taste, or polish. The author may ignore these.

- Word choice preferences.
- Optional formatting.
- Minor typos and small grammar issues (group these — do not list one per line).
- Suggestions that come from your style, not from a defect in theirs.

### Counting rules

- A review with **only nits** means the work is good — say so plainly.
- A review where **everything is Must fix** is almost always wrong; recheck whether the items are really blocking.
- Resist inflation: if a Should fix would be ignored at zero cost, it's a nit.
- Resist deflation: if a "nit" affects whether the reader trusts the work, it's Should fix.

### What every issue must include

1. **Location** — line number, section title, or quoted phrase.
2. **The problem** — one sentence, specific.
3. **Direction** — what good would look like, not a full rewrite.

Issues missing any of the three are not yet ready to send.
