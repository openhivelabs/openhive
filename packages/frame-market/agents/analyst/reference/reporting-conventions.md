## Reporting conventions

How to report numbers so a careful reader can trust them and an executive reader can act on them.

### Always report effect size, not just direction

- "Revenue went up" is useless. "Revenue rose 12% YoY ($1.2M → $1.34M)" is actionable.
- For comparisons, report **absolute and relative**: "down 800 units (−14%)".
- For rates, name the denominator: "23% of paying users" beats "23% of users" beats "23%".

### Sample size and uncertainty

- Always state n. If n < 30, soften: "in this small sample…".
- For survey data, name the sampling frame; convenience samples don't generalise.
- Distinguish **statistical significance** from **practical significance** — a real but tiny effect is still tiny.
- When you have a confidence interval, report it; when you don't, don't fake one.

### Time and seasonality

- Specify the period: "Q3 2025 vs Q3 2024" beats "this quarter vs last".
- For volatile metrics, prefer **trailing windows** (T7, T30) over single-day snapshots.
- Flag seasonality before reporting trend: a December spike isn't a trend.

### Anomalies and outliers

- Report them separately. Do not let a single outlier drive the headline number.
- If you exclude outliers, say so and show the result with and without.
- A "data quality" anomaly (bug, double-count) is a finding in itself — surface it.

### Causation language

- Use **correlation** language ("associated with", "tracks with") unless you have an experimental design.
- Reserve "caused", "drove", "led to" for cases with a real causal chain (RCT, natural experiment, mechanism).

### Format of findings

1. **Headline** — one sentence, one number.
2. **Numbered findings** — 3–7 items, each with the supporting figure.
3. **Caveats** — sample size, time window, known anomalies.
4. **Takeaway** — one final sentence: what to do or watch.

### Things to never do

- Round selectively to make a number look better.
- Compare across changed definitions without flagging the change.
- Bury the denominator.
- Report "average" when median or distribution would be more honest.
